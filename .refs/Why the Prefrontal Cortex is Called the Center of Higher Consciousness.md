## Why do we say the prefrontal cortex is "the center of our higher consciousness," and what does it actually mean?

### The Strange Case of Phineas Gage

We usually start discussing this with Phineas Gage. In 1848, during a work accident, an iron bar pierced through his head, damaging his frontal lobe. The frontal lobe is involved in what we know as executive functions. Like a company executive, this means they are responsible for managing everything from top to bottom. And this description is basically accurate.

The story of Phineas Gage is almost legendary. There are many exaggerations in descriptions of him. This passage from his doctor, John Harlow, is often quoted:

> Before the injury, although not schooled, he possessed a well-balanced mind, and was looked upon by those who knew him as a shrewd, smart businessman, very energetic and persistent in executing all his plans of operation. In this regard, his mind was radically changed, so decidedly that his friends and acquaintances said he was "no longer Gage."

Gage survived the injury, but it changed him. At least, that's part of the story. It is said he became aggressive, started gambling, and generally exhibited inappropriate behavior. Frontal lobe damage can lead to such behaviors, which is likely why many are happy to attribute these actions to Phineas Gage. "He was no longer Gage" is the moral of this story: without the frontal lobe, you are no longer "you."

However, we have a better story: Rosemary Kennedy, the sister of JFK.

### The Lobotomized Sister

Walter Freeman II was an American doctor who pioneered the reckless surgery known as the frontal lobotomy. He drove around in his car, now known as the "lobotomobile," and used an ice pick through the patients' eyes to damage their frontal lobes, believing it could cure various psychological illnesses.

Rosemary Kennedy underwent a lobotomy at the age of 23. Before Freeman became reckless with his ice pick, he had a true professional perform the surgery. This passage is often quoted in her father's biography:

> The instrument Dr. Watts used looked like a butter knife. He swung it up and down to cut brain tissue. "We put an instrument inside," he said. As Dr. Watts cut, Dr. Freeman asked Rosemary some questions. For example, he asked her to recite the Lord's Prayer or sing "God Bless America" or count backwards... "We estimated how far to cut based on her responses." When Rosemary began to become incoherent, they stopped.

Rosemary's mental state regressed to that of a two-year-old, and she was quickly hidden away from the public.

Here we have two stories of individuals who suffered frontal lobe damage—one accidental, one intentional. I share these stories because they are about real people having to face the consequences of damage to the area of the brain known as the "seat of our higher consciousness."

Let's look deeper.

### Meta-Reinforcement Learning

The Prefrontal Cortex (PFC) is the anterior part of the frontal lobe. In a brilliant 2018 paper³ published by DeepMind, the PFC was suggested to play the role of a "Meta-Reinforcement Learning system." This sounds complex, but we can learn a lot by deconstructing it.

Reinforcement learning is about learning based on the consequences of your actions. Does pressing the red button give me an electric shock? Then I won't press it. Does pressing the green button give me ice cream? I'll press it until dawn! Meta-reinforcement learning is the same, but it applies to the process of learning through consequences itself.

First, we must introduce another brain structure: the Striatum. A simple way to understand it (trust me, you need the simple version) is that it formulates rules based on the consequences of your actions. If there is ice cream nearby, then you eat it. So, if you want to simplify things enough to annoy your neuroscience friends, think of the striatum as where your habits reside.

Dopamine is used in the striatum to update these "rules." Did you get two scoops of ice cream instead of one after pressing the green button? That's better than expected! Your striatal synapses will quickly be flooded with dopamine, and your "action schema" will update so that you are more likely to press that green button in the future. This is achieved by changing the strength of synaptic connections.

What DeepMind proposed in their paper is that the striatum can "train" the PFC to generalize these behavioral rules. Meta-reinforcement learning can also be called "learning how to learn," because that's what it's about. Our prefrontal cortex allows us to take what we've learned and apply it to new situations. This means we don't have to rely solely on trial and error; we can learn how to learn things we've never encountered before.

Grand strategies, personal principles, and exploring unknown territories: all of this stems from our PFC's ability to generalize and abstract. This alone should earn it a place as the seat of higher consciousness.

As you might expect, planning and impulse control are related. They both fall under the category of "executive function." What should you do? What should you not do? When? How? The term "cognitive control" is also frequently used in this context.

### The Great Escape

In *The Executive Brain*⁴, neuroscientist Elkhonon Goldberg opens with a story about his escape from the Soviet Union.

> Attempting to leave the country required a complex plan, with no guarantee of success. To get out, I had to outsmart the Soviet Union. I knew my frontal lobes would be under immense pressure over the coming months.

He ended up intentionally messing up his PhD thesis defense so he would be less important and thus escape scrutiny. As he pointed out, delaying gratification for the pursuit of a higher goal was something he needed his frontal lobes for. He also shared three analogies often used to describe the PFC: the CEO, the Conductor, and the General.

The behaviors we tend to call intentional are precisely the behaviors we now associate with the PFC. Whether this is philosophically accurate on a larger scale doesn't matter to us. With the power of my PFC, I can choose not to eat ice cream because I have a long-term goal involving looking great at the beach, and even though my striatum is screaming _if there is ice cream nearby, then you eat it_, I can override this habitual impulse because it doesn't align with my plan.

### Closing Remarks

Aren't you just the sum of your past decisions? Of course, there are others involved and countless events, but when we talk about personality and the essence of a person, we're really talking about the choices they've made. Those failed or successful plans. The habits they tried to overcome. What they chose to ignore and what they chose to focus on. We're talking about their frontal lobes. They should be called the seat of our higher consciousness because they join us in pursuing our greatest goals and trying to handle our most embarrassing shortcomings.

Phineas Gage, Rosemary Kennedy, and countless others suffered the consequences of frontal lobe damage. So don't take your frontal lobes for granted.

Perhaps you understand now why I said Rosemary's story is better: her tragedy stemmed from Freeman's use of _his_ frontal lobe. He intentionally had his assistant destroy her frontal lobe in pursuit of the higher goals of fame and respect from his peers. With great power comes great responsibility. So use your abilities wisely.

Look at this important paper (https://www.jneurosci.org/content/37/40/9603.abstract), which argues that the PFC is not part of the foundation of consciousness at all (also check out various responses to this article that refute its view).

First, from functionalically speaking, the PFC is important for formulating plans, conducting abstract cognition not necessarily based on perceptual imagery, etc. Without your PFC, it would be difficult to lead a normal human life.

The argument in the linked paper is this: if you look at what happens when various parts of the brain are damaged (stroke, disease, injury, etc.), only damage to the more posterior parts of the brain seems to lead to a decrease in consciousness itself. For example, damage the visual cortex, and you lose aspects of the entire visual experience, such as no longer being able to experience half of the visual field, or you can experience it but without color or motion, and so on.

Meanwhile, damage to the PFC does lead to impaired behavior, cognition, etc., but does not seem to lead to the loss of experiential content. According to the linked paper, patients with PFC lesions seem to have roughly the same experience as normal people—but they are functionally impaired.

The counter-argument from the "PFC is necessary for consciousness" faction is that due to its connectivity, local lesions of the PFC can be compensated for by the rest of the PFC. So it is part of the foundation of consciousness, but in a very plastic, multi-realizable way.

In short, this is a massive debate. Some consciousness scientists believe the PFC is crucial for consciousness—others believe it is merely incidental.
